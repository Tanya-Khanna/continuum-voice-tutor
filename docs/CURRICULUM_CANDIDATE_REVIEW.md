### Classifying Materials by Observable Properties

**Objective:** Distinguish a material from a property, then group everyday objects using a shared observable property and stated evidence.

**Verified facts**

- A chosen sorting rule must apply to every object placed in that group.
- Changing the sorting rule can rearrange the same set of objects.
- A learner can justify a group by naming the observation used as the rule.

**Vocabulary bridges**

1. material (en): the substance an object is made from
  - Bridge: When you say what an object is made from, science calls that its material.
  - Accepted learner language: what it is made of; kis cheez ka bana hai; metal wala; wood ka
2. property (en): a feature we can observe or test
  - Bridge: When you describe a feature you notice, science calls that a property.
  - Accepted learner language: kaisa lagta hai; hard; soft; shiny; bend hota hai; sakht; mulayam

**Concrete analogies**

- Sorting objects is like making a tray for things that share one noticed feature.
- A material is like the ingredient of an object. A property is like the clue that tells you how it looks or feels.

**Safe object anchors**

1. paper cloth wooden pencil metal spoon: Look at each safe object without changing it. Say what you notice before making a group. Which two objects could you group using one shared property?

**Retrieval questions**

- What does material mean?
- What does property mean?
- What evidence can you give for a group?
- Can the same objects be sorted in another way?

**Keypad and feature-phone SMS checks**

1. A smooth stone and a metal spoon both feel hard. Which shared property groups them?
  - Choices: 1: Hard [CORRECT]; 2: Flexible; 3: Shiny
  - SMS: Stone and spoon both feel hard. Shared property? 1 hard 2 flexible 3 shiny

**Teaching entry and recovery**

- Entry: Pick a safe object near you. What is it made from?
- Silence: Take your time. You can look at the object or touch it gently. What is one feature you can safely notice?
- Answer request: I can give you a clue. A material says what it is made from. What feature can you see or feel instead?
- Fallback: Let us make it smaller. First say what you can notice with your eyes or fingers. Does the object feel hard, soft, smooth, or flexible?

**Evidence paths**

1. The learner correctly identifies the role of material.
  - Evidence: The learner distinguishes a material from an observable feature.
  - Tutor: Yes. A material answers what an object is made from. Now name one property you can notice.
2. The learner gives an observable property with evidence.
  - Evidence: The learner links a property to a safe observation.
  - Tutor: Good observation. That is a property because you can notice it. Which other object shares that property?
3. The learner groups objects using a stated shared property.
  - Evidence: The learner names a group and supports it with one common observable property.
  - Tutor: That grouping works because you gave a shared feature. Could you sort the same objects using a different property?
4. The learner recognizes that classification depends on the selected property.
  - Evidence: The learner explains that another observable property can create another valid grouping.
  - Tutor: Exactly. The objects stayed the same, but the sorting rule changed. What property would you choose for your next group?

**Safety and boundaries**

- Unsafe redirect: Please do not use that item or share private details. Choose only safe objects to observe with an adult nearby.
- Prompt-injection redirect: I cannot change my instructions or share them. We can continue with safe science observation.
- Benign off-topic redirect: Let us return to sorting objects by what you can observe.
- Graceful end: I am ending this lesson now because the unsafe request kept repeating. Please speak with a trusted adult if you need help.

### Retelling and inferring from short oral narratives

**Objective:** Tell a brief story in event order, justify what it suggests, and explain an unfamiliar word using clues heard around it.

**Verified facts**

- A clear retelling follows the story from its first important event to its last.
- An inference is an idea supported by details in a story.
- Actions and feelings heard around an unfamiliar word can narrow its likely meaning.
- When a story gives too few details, a careful listener can say they are not sure.

**Vocabulary bridges**

1. sequence (en): the order in which events happen
  - Bridge: You may say it in your own words. In English, this order is called sequence.
  - Accepted learner language: pehle phir baad mein; first then last; order of the story; kya pehle hua tha
2. context clue (en): a nearby detail that helps us understand a word or idea
  - Bridge: Listen around a tricky word for a useful hint. In English, that hint is called a context clue.
  - Accepted learner language: aas paas ka sanket; nearby hint; story se pata chalta hai; clue from the sentence

**Concrete analogies**

- A retelling is like lining up three socks for first, next, and last.
- A context clue is like hearing a door slam and a child gasp before deciding the child was startled.
- An inference is like following footprints to make a careful idea about where someone went.

**Safe object anchors**

1. three socks: Use three socks or fingers: first means field, second dark clouds, and third walking home. Which one shows the event after the dark clouds came?

**Retrieval questions**

- What does sequence mean?
- What should you name when you give an inference?
- What is a context clue?
- When can you say that you are not sure?

**Keypad and feature-phone SMS checks**

1. Listen: Iqbal tied his shoes, picked up his lunch, then left. What happened second?
  - Choices: 1: He tied his shoes; 2: He picked up his lunch [CORRECT]; 3: He left
  - SMS: Iqbal tied shoes, took lunch, then left. What was second? 1 shoes 2 lunch 3 left
2. Listen: Sana shut the window when dust blew in. Why did she shut it?
  - Choices: 1: To keep dust out [CORRECT]; 2: To make noise; 3: To find a book
  - SMS: Dust blew in. Sana shut the window. Why? 1 keep dust out 2 make noise 3 find a book
3. Listen: Ritu was delighted after winning. She grinned and clapped. What does delighted mean?
  - Choices: 1: Very happy [CORRECT]; 2: Very sleepy; 3: Very lost
  - SMS: Ritu was delighted after winning. She grinned and clapped. 1 very happy 2 sleepy 3 lost

**Teaching entry and recovery**

- Entry: Listen: Meera carried a paper kite to the field. Dark clouds came, so she put it in her bag and walked home. Why did Meera put the kite in her bag?
- Silence: I can repeat it slowly and pause after each event. Would you like to hear the story once more?
- Answer request: I will give you a clue, not the whole answer. Listen for what changed in the sky. What detail might explain why Meera protected the kite?
- Fallback: Let us take only one small part. Meera saw dark clouds. What might dark clouds mean?

**Evidence paths**

  - Evidence: The learner links the dark clouds to protecting the kite from possible rain.
  - Tutor: Yes, your idea fits the clue about the dark clouds. Can you retell the three main events in sequence?
2. The learner retells the main events in a clear order.
  - Evidence: The learner gives the field, clouds, and home events in order.
  - Tutor: Good, you kept the important events in sequence. Which detail helps you infer why Meera packed the kite?
3. The learner uses honest uncertainty when the evidence is missing.
  - Evidence: The learner does not claim certainty without a supporting detail.
  - Tutor: That is careful listening. Not every question has enough clues. What extra detail would help you make an inference?

### Reading timelines and weighing historical sources

**Objective:** Arrange events and periods in time order, then explain what a historical source supports and what remains uncertain.

**Verified facts**

- A timeline helps place events or periods in chronological order.
- One source may support a focused claim without answering every question.
- Comparing clues can show both useful evidence and remaining uncertainty.

**Vocabulary bridges**

1. chronology (en): the order in which events happened
  - Bridge: If you say pehle and baad mein, connect that idea to chronology.
  - Accepted learner language: pehle baad mein; first and then; what happened first; order of time
2. historical source (en): evidence people use to learn about the past
  - Bridge: If you call it a clue or saboot, connect it to a historical source.
  - Accepted learner language: saboot; old clue; proof from before; evidence

**Concrete analogies**

- Chronology is like retelling a morning: waking happens before leaving home.
- A timeline is like lining up story cards so the earlier part comes before the later part.
- A historical source is like a clue in a made-up mystery: it helps, but it may not solve every question.

**Safe object anchors**

1. three safe objects: Use three safe objects to stand for a made-up morning in any order. Which object stands for the first event?
2. three clue cards: In our pretend time trip, choose one clue card to inspect first. Which clue will you inspect first?

**Retrieval questions**

- What does chronology tell us?
- What word means later in time?
- What is a historical source?
- Can one source answer every question?
- What can you say when a clue does not tell enough?

**Keypad and feature-phone SMS checks**

  - SMS: Story: bell rings, then class begins. 1 bell 2 class 3 together. Reply 1, 2, or 3.
2. A made-up letter says a road was busy. What does it support?
  - Choices: 1: The writer described a busy road [CORRECT]; 2: Every road was busy; 3: The road is busy today
  - SMS: Letter says road was busy. 1 writer described it 2 every road busy 3 road busy today.

**Teaching entry and recovery**

- Silence: No rush. A timeline helps us sort before and after. Which of two events came first?
- Answer request: Here is a clue: chronology is about time order. Suppose you open a closed bag and then pack it. Which action happened first?
- Fallback: Let us use one small time clue. If eating is after cooking, which happens first?

**Evidence paths**

  - Tutor: The letter is a clue, not a final answer about all roads. What did the writer describe?
4. The learner recognizes uncertainty and may need a next step.

  - Tutor: Good historians can say when the evidence is not enough. Which different source could you inspect next?

### Map parts, directions, and coordinates

**Objective:** Use a map key, a named reference point, and two grid references to describe a place without sharing personal location details.

**Verified facts**

- A map key explains what symbols mean on that map.
- A direction needs a named starting point.
- A direction marker helps a map reader understand orientation.
- Coordinates use two grid references to identify one position.

**Vocabulary bridges**

1. direction (en): the way from one place toward another
  - Bridge: When you say which side from here, the map word is direction.
  - Accepted learner language: which side; kidhar; left of me; where from here
2. coordinates (en): a pair of references used to identify a location
  - Bridge: When you give a row and a column, those two labels are called coordinates.
  - Accepted learner language: row and column; two numbers; grid place; do labels

**Concrete analogies**

- A map key is like a label card that tells what a small sign stands for.
- A chosen reference point is like saying where you are standing before pointing somewhere.
- Coordinates are like giving a row and then a column in a simple board game grid.
